Neurotransmitters

Neurotransmitters are chemical substances produced and stored in synaptic vesicles in the soma. Soma is the term for a neuron's cell body. Between the axon of one neuron and the dendrites of another is a tiny gap.

Neurotransmitters travel down the axon to the axon terminal where they are released into a tiny gap. Most of the communication in the brain takes place across these tiny gaps, or synapses.

Messages from a neuron “hop" over the synaptic gap to deliver the message from one cell to the next. This process is shown in the following illustration.

Neurons generate an action potential when neurotransmitters' excitatory inputs exceed inhibitory inputs. This brief electrochemical charge in the axon depolarizes the cell and causes a domino-type of effect of depolarization all the way down the axon.

After binding of a neurotransmitter to a dendrite occurs and action potentials are fired, neurotransmitters are removed from the Synapse by the process of reuptake, or they are destroyed by enzymes.

For the synapse to work properly, six basic events need to happen:

Production of neurotransmitters

Storage of neurotransmitters

Release of neurotransmitters

Binding of neurotransmitters

Generation of a new action potential

Removal of neurotransmitters from the synapse

It’s time to learn about Schwann cells

Schwann cells

Schwann cells are found along the length of the axon. These cells wrap around the axon with small gaps between each cell. Axons end in a series or group of synaptic knobs. These structures stimulate other nerves or a target organ. This causes physical responses.

For example, an arm may move or a lid may close on an eye based on these synapses.

Different sets of neurons use different types of neurotransmitters.

A group of neurons that
communicate with one another using the same neurotransmitter is called a neurotransmitter system. There are seven major neurotransmitters, let’s learn more about them:

Acetylcholine

Was the first compound to be identified as a neurotransmitter. This neurotransmitter is used by neurons to slow the heartbeat and activate the digestive system.

Problems with this system are linked to memory loss and eventual loss of mental powers (as exhibited in Alzheimer’s).

Norepinephrine

These systems affect a person's mood, learning, wakefulness and arousal. Changes in this system have been linked to depression.

Serotonin

Is similar to norepinephrine in that it affects both sleep and mood.

Serotonin has been linked to aggression and impulsive behaviors. Malfunction of the serotonin feedback system is suspected to be a major contributor to mood and appetite problems.

Dopamine

Is a neurotransmitter that is important to movement.

Problems with the dopamine system are thought to be associated with the perceptual, thought and emotional disturbances of schizophrenia.

Dopamine also plays a role in the rewarding properties of many drugs, like cocaine.

GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid)

Is similar to norepinephrine in that it affects both sleep and mood.

Serotonin has been linked to aggression and impulsive behaviors. Malfunction of the serotonin feedback system is suspected to be a major contributor to mood and appetite problems.

Glutamate

Is used more by neurons than any of the other neurotransmitters. Glutamate enables the brain to reinforce its synaptic connections.

Glutamate can cause neurons to die if they are overused.

Endorphins

Endorphins, in this context, are often substances called opiates. Opiates like morphine or heroin, can relieve pain and produce intense feelings of happiness.

Neurotransmitters

Neurotransmitters affect a person’s mood, appetite and ability to reason.

Problems with our dopamine system can be linked to schizophrenia. Complications with our serotonin system have been linked to depression.
“It is important for psychologists and psychiatrists to know what neurons are being affected to prescribe the appropriate type of medication if therapeutic drugs are being used.”

Legal drugs such as alcohol, therapeutic drugs (those prescribed for sufferers of schizophrenia, depression, etc) and illegal drugs (cocaine and LSD) all affect our physiology and behavior. Many of the effects of the drugs can also affect us on the synaptic level.
