Disorders of the Nervous System

What did Muhammad Ali and Michael J. Fox have in common?

Both of these men were diagnosed with a nervous system disorder that affects their behaviors.

Muhammad Ali was once a boxer who performed with great precision and skill. The first indicators that something was wrong were infrequent problems maintaining his balance, deteriorating writing, slurred speech and fatigue. In 1984 Muhammad Ali was diagnosed with Parkinson’s disorder. Initially, Ali seemed to have responded well to treatment.
Unfortunately his condition worsened and it was later revealed that his disease had been caused by too many blows to the head from his boxing career.

Michael J. Fox is a Canadian-American actor who was diagnosed with Parkinson's disease in 1991. Michael J. Fox has been an advocate for Parkinson's disease research and has raised significant funds for the Michael J. Fox Foundation, which is dedicated to finding a cure for the disease.

The first question to address about the nervous system is:

What is a neuron and what does it do?

Neurons

Every action in the body is somehow represented in the nervous system. The nervous system is a mechanism to gain access to information about what is going on inside and outside the body and how to respond accordingly. It is a network composed of cells that work together that allows us to see, hear, taste, think and move. That cells are called Neurons. A neuron is a specialized cell. It consists of a cell body, dendrites, and an axon, and is responsible for receiving, processing, and transmitting information.

Messengers of the Body

Neurons are specialized cells in the body that send and receive messages. Most neurons are similar in structure but they can vary in shape, size, length and function.
For example, the neurons in your spine are longer than the neurons in your brain. Glial Cells are responsible for holding neurons together, for directing growth, keeping the chemical environment stable, secreting chemicals to help repair damage and modify the signals that are sent between neurons.
Cells contain a nucleus (with the genetic material of the cell) and mitochondria (cellular organelles that are found outside the nucleus). Neurons follow a structure similar to other cells.
The neuron has an outer membrane that allows some substances to pass through while others are denied.
Neurons have mitochondria that turn oxygen and glucose into energy.

Neuron has several parts, including:

Dendrites: receive signals from other neurons and conduct them to the cell body.

Cell body (Soma): the central part of the neuron that contains the nucleus and other organelles.

Axon: a long, slender projection that carries signals away from the cell body to other neurons or muscles.

Myelin sheath: a fatty insulation around the axon that increases the speed of nerve impulses.

Terminal buttons: the tips of the axon that release neurotransmitters to communicate with other neurons or muscles.

Nodes of Ranvier: short gaps in the myelin sheath along the axon that allow for faster conduction of nerve impulses.

Action Potential

Neurons may have different shapes and sizes but they all have long, thin fibers that reach outward from the cell body. When these fibers get close to other neurons, communication between the cell bodies can occur.
The fibers that extend from the cell body are called dendrites and axons. An Action Potential is the communication signal between neurons that begins with an electrochemical pulse that shoots down the Axon.
Once a cell has fired, there is a short recovery period in which the cell cannot fire again. This recovery time is called the Refractory Period.
When the neurons fire, the Dendrites in the next cell can detect the message and pass the signal on to their cell body.
How does a signal travel between neurons? As a vibration. As an electrochemical pulse.

How Biology Affects Psychology

Biological factors are closely related to behavior and mental processes. How biology has an impact on behavior is the question that scientists attempt to answer through biological psychology.
Biological psychology is the study of cells, genes, and organs of the body and the physical, hormonal, and chemical changes involved in behavior and mental processes.
It has been argued that the environment influences behavior and mental processes, but that biology is the main contributing factor.
This means that some people simply have a predisposition to respond a certain way.
