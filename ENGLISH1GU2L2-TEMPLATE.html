Adjectives

An adjective is a word that modifies or describes a noun or a pronoun. Adjectives enhance our writing by allowing us to describe a thought, an experience, or a situation in greater detail. When we write a sentence, we want people to imagine what we are describing. We want to paint a mental picture that the reader can "see".

Lesson Keywords

Adjectives:

Words that describe or modify nouns or pronouns.

Relative Pronouns

Words that introduce relative clauses in sentences.

Relative clauses

Clauses that provide additional information about a noun or pronoun in a sentence.

Adjectives occur in several forms

Nouns

Nouns become adjectives when you add an ending like -ly, -ish, -en.

EX. Manly, childish, silken

Numbers

Numbers are considered adjectives.

EX. Two apples

Pronouns and Articles

Demonstrative and possessive pronouns and articles are considered adjectives.

EX. This paper, his personality, the movie

Multiple

More than one adjective is often used to describe something.

EX. The pretty green sweater

Compound

Two adjectives are often combined to use as one adjective and are usually hyphenated.

EX. The mild-mannered man

The Rules

Adjective Rules

Adjectives modify nouns: We have already discussed this rule above. Adjectives follow the verb ''to be'' when it modifies the noun preceding the verb.

EX. “Thomas was nervous.”

In this sentence, nervous describes Thomas

play_arrow

An adjective always follows the following types of verbs when it modifies the noun before the verb: sense, appearance, feel, taste, smell, seem, appear, and look.

EX. “Leslie's voice sounds harsh. Harsh is the adjective in the sentence. It describes Leslie's voice.”

Let's review quickly. Adjectives, in general, modify nouns. Adjectives also modify nouns that precede the verb "to be" and verbs of the senses. In addition, adjectives help us write clear and descriptive sentences. This allows us to demonstrate our idea, concept, situation, etc. with more detail.

This keeps those who read our sentences more interested in what we are writing about because of the expressive words used to portray our thoughts.

ADJECTIVE CLAUSES

play_arrow

Remember when you learned that nouns can be transformed into a noun clause?

This is also the case for adjectives.

Adjective clauses contain a subject and a verb, but serve as an adjective in a sentence. They are connected to the sentence by a relative pronoun.

Adjective clauses are also known as relative clauses. The terms are interchangeable.

Here are some examples. Notice how each italicized adjective/relative clause has its own subject and verb and describes a noun.

The man who just moved in enjoys gardening.

This is the bird that built a nest on our windowsill.

This is the field where Phillip scored the winning goal.
