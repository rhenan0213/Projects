City-States

The Mesopotamian civilizations were organized in city-states, which typically were walled cities built of mud that included government buildings and homes. The land surrounding the city was called the state, and it was here that people developed agriculture. The people in the city-states shared a common culture, language, and religion, but they were not ruled by a single king or warlord. Each city-state had its own leader, and competition arose among the communities. Wars over food and other resources were not uncommon. The most important building in the city-state was the ziggurat, the temple to the gods.

The Sumerians were instrumental in the invention of the wheel, plow, and an early written language called cuneiform. They also created irrigation processes that allowed them to control the amount of water reaching their crops. These vast networks made it possible for communities to rise, grow, and prosper in the Fertile Crescent.

Government in Mesopotamia

Each Mesopotamian city-state was ruled by a king or warlord who created and enforced laws and collected taxes. People believed that the king was selected by the gods, and he often served both as king and religious leader. Other men and women were permitted to act as priests and priestesses, but generally, it was the king who ruled the city-state and acted as the mediator with the gods.

The king protected the people within the walled cities from invaders, as well as protecting the surplus used for trading. The people were highly taxed by the king, and they were expected to donate the food they produced to the king. He then ensured that the gods were happy by organizing sacrifices and offering food to them twice each day. The remaining food was given back to the people, distributed in a manner to assure that everyone got enough to eat.

Life and Society in Mesopotamia

The irrigation systems which were essential for agriculture required a great amount of physically hard work, complicated construction and the most important of all mobilization of sufficient labor force and good organization. Thus artificial irrigation eventually led to formation of larger communities and social stratification into three classes: nobility, free citizens and slaves. Nobility, priesthood and a priest-king as supreme religious and secular ruler were responsible for the construction, organization and maintenance of irrigation systems. Priest-kings called en, lugal or ensi were viewed as representatives of the city's patron god who belonged to all the land of the city. Priest-kings lived in the temples which were both religious and administrative centers of a particular irrigation system unit. Around the temples developed cities as administrative, religious and trade centers resulting in the emergence of Sumerian city-states also referred as the temple-states because of the leading role of priesthood in society.

The Sumerian cities were inhabited by nobility executing the king's will and orders through a well organized administrative apparatus. The Sumerian priest-kings collected taxes usually in goods to sustain the governmental and administrative organization, while the Sumerian priest invented writing - the Cuneiform script to simplify state administration and trading. Besides priesthood and nobility, Sumerian city population consisted of merchants, craftsmen and others who were mostly engaged in trade. The middle class of merchants and craftsmen in Mesopotamia was very strong and relatively independent. Farmers, shepherds, fishermen and hunters in Mesopotamia lived in the city surroundings and were the lower class of the Mesopotamian society. They took care of the food supply of the cities and were mostly poor but personally free. At the very bottom of social hierarchy in Mesopotamia were the slaves who were mostly prisoners of war. Slaves could be bought or sold but they were allowed to marry and to have families. Special positions in both administration and economy had the scribes.

Economic Life

As the early societies grew more adept at agriculture, they actually had surplus food to trade with each other and with people and communities further away. The city-states started trading for products that they needed. At the same time, artisans were growing more skilled. As they became further gifted, they needed better ingredients with which to make the products. This need created a demand for foreign articles, and this was yet another reason for increased trade.

Mesopotamia and the resources traded

The people of Mesopotamia traded a variety of goods, including:

Agricultural products: Grains such as wheat and barley were grown in the fertile land of Mesopotamia and traded with neighboring regions.

Livestock:: Sheep, goats, and cattle were raised for their meat, milk, and hides, and were also traded with other cultures.

Metals: Copper, tin, and iron were mined and used to make weapons, tools, and jewelry. They were also traded with other civilizations.

Textiles: Mesopotamian weavers were known for their skill in making cloth from wool and linen, which were then traded with other cultures.

Spices and herbs: Mesopotamia had access to a variety of spices and herbs, including cinnamon, cumin, and coriander, which were traded with other civilizations.

These goods were traded through a network of merchants and traders, both within Mesopotamia and with neighboring regions. The Tigris and Euphrates rivers served as important trade routes, connecting Mesopotamia to the Mediterranean world and beyond.

Religion

Have you ever wondered the bigger questions of the universe? Why is there suffering? Why do people die? Where do they go when they die?

These questions about life and death are timeless and are not unique to modern societies. In fact, the early people of Mesopotamia had the same questions, and the ancient Sumerians left an extensive record of their religious beliefs.

People in Sumer wanted to understand why the rivers flooded. The flooding was unpredictable, and they started to believe that the gods were angry with them. They built ziggurats as temples where the priests would offer sacrifices. A religion with many gods is called polytheism, while a religion with one god is called monotheism.

Each of the city-states of Sumer was ruled by a priest or group of priests who were believed to be chosen by and acting to satisfy the gods. There were seven major gods, and each of whom is tied to the Earth. The Sumerians worshiped gods of the Earth, sky, sun, moon, wind, fresh water, and salt (sea) water. Because the Sumerians believed that they were indebted to the gods for all they had, food was given to the gods. The priests would offer food to the gods twice each day, and it was the responsibility of the priests to assure that the remaining food was distributed among the people of the city-state, assuring that everyone was fed.

Social Structure

Every culture across time has developed a social structure. Many years ago, in America and Europe, children and women were considered property of their fathers or husbands. Some cultures have tightly controlled gender roles. Other cultures are more equal. What about the social structure in Sumeria?

The social structure of Sumeria was very strict. Men controlled the family, and they were even allowed to sell their family to settle any debt they had. Women had the right to buy and sell land only if the man was absent.

Innovation in Agriculture

Living in a land between two rivers was challenging, but it provided the Mesopotamian civilizations with fertile ground and an abundance of wild grains and nuts. Yet even though the soil was rich, one of the main problems for the early civilizations was flooding, something that not only threatened the crops and food supply of the Fertile Crescent, but also threatened the lives and survival of the people living in the region. Citizens learned to protect themselves from destruction by developing an irrigation system that provided a more constant supply of water required by the crops. This approach included digging irrigation trenches that would serve as the foundation for this system The farmers built irrigation gates that controlled the amount of water reaching the crops. The building of dams prevented the rivers from flooding and directed the water to the crops.

Written Language

To keep track of trading and taxes paid to the government, Sumerians developed a written language called cuneiform. It was a series of wedges written on clay tablets. Clay was very abundant because of the land between the two rivers, which was often muddy, thus providing a good supply of material for tablets.

Because of the abundant materials, artwork and written works have survived through the centuries. These tablets provide a lasting record of Mesopotamian cultures as each group recorded those elements of their society that they deemed important. And while each group focused on different elements of their society, almost all of Sumerian art reflected their land between the rivers.

The ancient Mesopotamian languages are still being studied, and archeologists and anthropologists continue to gain a better understanding of the culture. But there are records and art that provide a good understanding of the beliefs and customs of the time. For example, the Sumerians built ziggurats, elaborate stepped temples. As part of the construction of these structures, they included great clay tablets inscribed with stories they wished to tell and record. The most famous of these stories is The Epic of Gilgamesh.
