What is Geography?

A limited definition of geography is that it is the study of places and involves coloring maps. In this course, you will discover that geography is more about the relationship of people to their environments. In addition, geography includes the study of the "state of our world." The environment is a hot-button issue that we will discuss much more throughout this course.

The Five Themes of Geography


Theme 1: Movement

How are People and Places Connected?

Human beings occupy places unevenly across the face of the earth. Some live on farms or in the country; others live in towns, villages, or cities. Yet these people interact with each other; that is, they travel from one place to another, they communicate with each other, and they rely upon products, information, and ideas that come from beyond their immediate environment. The most visible pieces of evidence of global interdependence and the interaction of places are the transportation and communication lines that link every part of the world. These demonstrate that most people interact with other places almost every day of their lives.


Theme 2: Regions

What Makes and Area Similar to or Different from Others?

The simplest measure of geographic study is the region, an area with unifying characteristics. We are all familiar with regions showing the extent of their boundaries such as nations, states, countries, provinces, or cities; yet there are almost countless ways to define meaningful regions. Some are defined by one characteristic such as a government, a language group, a land form type, religion or even food.


Theme 3: Location

Where are Various Regions in the World?

Position on the earth's surface – absolute location and relative location – are two ways of describing the positions of people and places on the earth’s surface.

●ABSOLUTE LOCATION

Absolute location is the latitude and longitude coordinates of a specific place on the earth. If you know the latitude and longitude coordinates of a specific city, you can find it anywhere in the world. For example Miami, Florida has the coordinates of 25° 45' 42.12 N and 80° 11' 30.48 W. Or, in decimal degrees the location is +25.7617° and - 80.1918°.

●RELATIVE LOCATION

Relative location describes a place in relation to its geography and things around it. For example, Miami became a popular location for cruise lines due to it's prime access to the Caribbean islands.


Theme 4: Interaction

How Does Environment Affect Life in a Region

All places on the earth have advantages and disadvantages for human settlement. The main idea of this theme is the relationship between people and their natural environments.

High population densities have developed on flood plains, for example, where people could take advantage of fertile soils, water resources, and opportunities for river transportation.

On the other hand, population densities are usually low in deserts, where these natural resources are lacking.


Theme 5: Place

How Does Culture Affect Life in Specific Regions?

All places on the earth have distinctive characteristics that give them meaning and character and distinguish them from other places. Geographers generally describe places by their physical or human characteristics.

For example, Colorado can be described as a mountainous state, as it is replete with these physical features. Likewise, states such as Nebraska, Kansas and Wyoming can be described as the Plains States due to their flat landscapes.

Some parts of the U.S. are also identifiable from their populations, which may include various minority groups. For example, Texas is influenced by its large population of Mexican immigrants, and San Francisco is inhabited by many Asian immigrants.

Azimuthal Perspective Projections


Some classic azimuthal projections are perspective projections and can be produced geometrically. They can be visualized as projection of points on the sphere to the plane by shining rays of light from a light source (or point of perspective). Three projections, namely gnomonic, stereographic and orthographic can be defined based on the location of the perspective point or the light source

Gnomonic Projection (also known as Central or Gnomic Projection):
The point of perspective or the light source is located at the center of the globe in gnomonic projections. Great circles are the shortest distance between two points on the surface of the sphere (known as great circle route). Gnomonic projections map all great circles as straight lines, and such property makes these projections suitable for use in navigation charts.

Stereographic Projection:
In stereographic projections, the perspective point is located on the surface of globe directly opposite from the point of tangency of the plane. Points close to center point show great distortion on the map. Stereographic projection is a conformal projection, that is over small areas angles and therefore shapes are preserved. It is often used for mapping Polar Regions (with the source located at the opposite pole).

Orthographic Projection:
In orthographic projections, the point of perspective is at infinite distance on the opposite direction from the point of tangency. The light rays travel as parallel lines. The resulting map from this projection looks like a globe (similar to seeing Earth from deep space). There is great distortion towards the borders of the map.
